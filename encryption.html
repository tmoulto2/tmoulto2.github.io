Encryption is the process of encoding data to prevent unauthorized access. It plays a vital role in securing communications and sensitive information online.

Why Encryption Matters

  • Protects Sensitive Data: Keeps information safe from hackers and eavesdroppers during transmission or storage.
  • Supports Compliance: Meets regulatory standards like HIPAA, GDPR, and PCI-DSS.
  • Enables Secure Communication: Powers protocols like HTTPS, VPNs, and secure email exchanges.
  • Validates Identity: Digital certificates and signatures use encryption to prove authenticity and prevent tampering.

Common Types of Encryption

  • Symmetric Encryption
  • Asymmetric Encryption

Encryption Comparison

Feature Symmetric Encryption Asymmetric Encryption
Key Used Same key for encryption and decryption Public key for encryption, private key for decryption
Speed Faster Slower
Security Less secure if key is intercepted More secure due to key separation
Use Cases Encrypting files, disk storage, VPN traffic Email encryption, digital signatures, SSL/TLS
Common Algorithms AES, DES, Blowfish RSA, ECC, DSA
Key Length Typically 128–256 bits Typically 1024–4096 bits

Common Encryption Algorithms

  • AES (Advanced Encryption Standard): A widely used symmetric algorithm known for its speed and security, adopted by the U.S. government.
  • RSA: A popular asymmetric algorithm used in secure web traffic (HTTPS), emails, and digital signatures.
  • ECC (Elliptic Curve Cryptography): Offers strong security with smaller key sizes, making it efficient for mobile and IoT devices.
  • DES/3DES: Older symmetric methods now largely replaced by AES due to security concerns.
